Music Lessons and the Community

As a doctoral student at George Mason University, I worked with a team to conduct interviews and analyze qualitative data for MusicLink Foundation. From the foundation's website:
Any child who has musical potential deserves the opportunity to nurture this talent to its full extent. Many children lack the chance to receive music lessons due to financial need. The MusicLink Foundation reaches out to low-income families by linking these students with professional music teachers willing to reduce their fee to make the lessons more affordable for the child.”

Methodology

I interviewed former students, teachers, and parents who were past users of the MusicLink service.
During the remote interviews, I recorded digital audio, took field notes, and made affinity notes.

I transcribed the interviews from the digital recorders using initial line-by-line coding. Then I added marginal notes to describe the sections of the interview and develop categories and subcategories that might come to expose the greater narrative of the data. I also pulled out quotes that were compelling or pertinent. 
​
I created post-it notes with the information I gained from the constant comparison and focused coding. Each participant had a unique color of post-it. These were put on an Affinity Wall. 
​Next, I rearranged the codes to form clusters. I identified nine clear patterns for the data narrative. Here are the two most actionable key findings:

Key Finding 1

MusicLink was specifically mentioned several times as being the primary reason music was able to be in the lives of some of the families. This would not be possible without the important connection of the teacher and student. The teacher/student dynamic leads to motivation, trust, organizational strategies, and ultimately a life-long love of music. MusicLink should continue to place emphasis on the strong bond between teacher and student.
I didn’t like her, so I took from her and then I quit... I started again when I was 9 and I had that teacher then all the way through college.”

Key Finding 2

​The next key finding is useful as a marketing device. There are some documented longitudinal effects from taking music lessons beyond the appreciation for a variety of music styles. Participants reported improved creativity/self-expression, self-discipline, recognition of talent in others, persistence, and self-confidence all attributed to MusicLink lessons.
...sometimes your impact, you don’t realize for 20 years.”

Conclusion

MusicLink’s process of identifying and developing musical talent has been working well. One way to keep students enrolled is to make sure they have the right teacher. Another way is to continue to celebrate the life-long skills they develop along the way.
