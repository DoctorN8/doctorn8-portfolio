Predicting Revenue with YouTube Subscribers

Linear Regression Analysis in R Studio

While scrolling through YouTube I noticed something strange: Nationally recognized Art Museums rarely have more than a few hundred subscribers.

"As of 2022, the media personality Logan Paul has more than 23 Million channel subscribers."

If you have never heard of Logan Paul, but you've heard of the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum, you understand the problem.

Methodology

First, I created a database with data from CauseIQ. Then, I added YouTube Channel Subscribers as a variable. Using R Studio, I ran a linear regression to predict Revenue based on Subscribers.

It didn't look like much, but I wanted to see if the math checked out... It did not. Ok, let's look at the distributions.

Those are both really skewed! Time to transform the data. With a little math magic (logarithmic transformation), we get a more normal distribution.

Key Findings

"The model explains about 35% of the variation in revenue, which means that there are likely other factors that influence revenue as well."

Based on the model output, a one-unit increase in the log number of subscribers is associated with a 0.27397 increase in the log revenue. Since both variables are in logarithmic form, this implies a power law relationship. Specifically, for every doubling of the number of subscribers, the revenue is expected to increase by a factor of approximately 2.

Example: If a channel has 1,000 subscribers and generates $1,000, a similar channel with 2,000 subscribers would be expected to generate $2,347.

Conclusion

Based on the linear regression model, we can conclude that there is a positive relationship between subscribers and revenue. Specifically, for every doubling of subscribers, revenue is expected to increase by ~2.3x. However, marketing strategies, content difficulty, and target audience also play critical roles.
