Predicting Revenue with YouTube Subscribers

While scrolling through YouTube I noticed something strange: Nationally recognized Art Museums rarely have more than a few hundred subscribers. 
The media personality Logan Paul has more than 23 Million channel subscribers.”
If you have never heard of Logan Paul, but you've heard of the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum (Thanks, Netflix) you understand the problem.

Key Findings

To summarize, the model found that there is a positive relationship between revenue and subscribers, but there are likely other factors that influence revenue as well (Wait, what?). The errors in the model are normally distributed, which is good, but the variance of the errors is not consistent across the range of subscribers, which means that the model may not be making equally accurate predictions for all channels.

It's important to keep in mind that this model is just one way of looking at the relationship between revenue and subscribers on YouTube. There may be other variables that are important to consider as well, and this model may not be the best fit for all channels. However, by understanding the findings of this model, we can start to get a better understanding of how revenue and subscribers are related on YouTube.
The model explains about 35% of the variation in revenue, which means that there are likely other factors that influence revenue as well.”
Based on the model output, the relationship between the log number of subscribers and the log revenue of the channel is described by the estimated coefficient of 0.27397. This coefficient indicates that a one-unit increase in the log number of subscribers is associated with a 0.27397 increase in the log revenue of the channel, holding all other variables constant.

Since both variables are in logarithmic form, this implies that the relationship between the actual number of subscribers and the actual revenue is a power law relationship. Specifically, for every doubling of the number of subscribers, the revenue is expected to increase by a factor of approximately 2. As an example, if a channel has 1,000 subscribers and the museum generates $1,000 in revenue, then a similar museum with 2,000 YouTube subscribers would be expected to generate $2,000 x 2^(0.27397) = $2,347.

However, it is important to note that this relationship is based on the assumption that other factors that may affect the revenue are held constant. In reality, there may be other variables such as the Difficulty, video content, target audience, and marketing strategies that can also impact the revenue. Thus, the estimated relationship between the number of subscribers and the revenue should be interpreted with caution and may not be the only or the strongest factor that influences the revenue of a channel.

Conclusion

Based on the linear regression model, we can conclude that there is a positive relationship between the number of subscribers and the revenue generated by a YouTube channel. Specifically, for every doubling of the number of subscribers, the revenue is expected to increase by a factor of approximately 2, holding all other variables constant. The model also indicates that the number of subscribers is a significant predictor of the revenue, with a very small p-value.

​However, it is important to note that other factors such as the Difficulty, video content, target audience, and marketing strategies may also impact the revenue and should be taken into consideration when analyzing and predicting the revenue of a YouTube channel. Overall, the model provides a useful tool for analyzing and understanding the relationship between the number of subscribers and the revenue of a YouTube channel, but further research is needed to fully understand the complex factors that contribute to the success of a channel.
